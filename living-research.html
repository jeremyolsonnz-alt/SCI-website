Living Research

The published research examined 78 people across six countries. This page extends that work — inviting anyone living with spinal cord injury to answer the same kinds of questions, see their own results, and contribute anonymously to a growing picture of how people cope and where quality of life gaps are widest. The data below updates in real time as new contributions come in.

Take Part

Choose a Self-Reflection

Two tools, two angles on the same question — how are you coping, and how is your life going? Both end with a personal results page and an optional, anonymous contribution to the community data below. Both also ask the two open-ended questions from the original Deakin research: what has helped you cope most, and what has been hardest?

Intuitive · 5 minutes
Your Healing Style
Eleven questions about how you tend to get through hard days. At the end you receive a named healing style — Connected, Analytical, Autonomous, or Visionary — with practical suggestions. Built from general coping research applied to SCI.
What makes it different: Uses personality-style archetypes rather than research scales. Quicker, more intuitive, best if you want an immediate, relatable profile.
Begin
Research-Grounded · 7 minutes
Coping Strategies & Quality of Life
Maps your coping strategies directly to the five strategies of control measured in the original Deakin study (Heckhausen & Schulz, 1995), then assesses importance and satisfaction across eight life domains using the ComQol framework (Cummins, 1997).
What makes it different: Uses the same theoretical instruments as the published research. More detailed, more precise — shows how your results connect to the specific finding that predicted wellbeing.
Begin

The community pattern echoes the original Deakin finding: Compensatory Secondary Control — attributing setbacks to circumstance rather than self-blame — remains the highest-scoring strategy. In the published research (Olson & Cummins, 1999), it was the only strategy significantly associated with higher life satisfaction (β = .412, p < .01).

Emerging Pattern

People in the earlier stages lean more on direct effort and show higher self-blame. Over time, the protective "bad luck" reframing strengthens and self-blame decreases — consistent with the original finding that adaptation deepens with time.

The themes emerging from the community voices closely mirror the original qualitative findings (Olson, 2001): family and close relationships remain the most frequently cited source of coping, while loss of independence remains the most frequently cited difficulty — just as they were twenty-five years ago.

About this data: All results are anonymous and aggregated. No individual can be identified. Contributors opted in voluntarily after receiving their personal results. Open-ended responses are reviewed manually before appearing here. The sample is self-selected, not randomly sampled — interpret patterns as indicative rather than representative. The original Deakin research (N = 78, six countries) used validated instruments under institutional ethics approval; this community tool is a self-reflection exercise, not a clinical study.
