Part One

Hands-free harmonica
performance — 15 minutes

At a glance
Duration
15 minutes
Setup
Neck rack · mic · portable PA
Instrument
C harp · second position · key of G
Follows into
Guided Imagery Programme overview
Audience

Inpatients at spinal cord injury rehabilitation centres, including people with quadriplegia. Some participants may be early in their rehabilitation journey. The tone is warm, accessible, and demonstration-focused — not performative. The performance is designed to make the barrier to entry feel real and low, and to connect the act of playing to the respiratory benefit that underpins the whole programme.

Set structure
01
3 min
Open
One recognisable song — no introduction

Played straight, without preamble. Either Blowin' in the Wind (Dylan) or Heart of Gold (Neil Young) — played as a slow blues in G on single notes, melodic and unhurried. No talking. The neck rack is visible from the start; the absence of hand function is immediately apparent. Let the room adjust to what they are watching before speaking a word.

No backing track
02
5–6 min
Demonstration
Slow 12-bar blues — technique made visible

One sentence before the music starts: "Everything you just heard was breath, a small movement of the head, and the mouth. That's it — no hands. Let me show you what that actually looks like up close."

Backing track on. Slow 12-bar in G. Three techniques introduced in sequence:

  • Chugging — breath as rhythm, the train beat. Establishes the idea that breath drives the music.
  • Single notes with visible head movement between holes — slightly exaggerated so it reads from across the room.
  • Bending and throat vibrato — the emotional peak of the demonstration. Let the sound change. This is the sound people associate with blues harmonica.
Backing track on
03
2–3 min
Teach-back
Three notes — the beginning of the blues scale

Stop the backing track. Pause. Play holes 2-draw, 3-draw, 4-draw slowly. Three notes, rising. Pause. Play again.

"That's three notes. It's the beginning of the blues scale. It sounds like music immediately, because it is. You don't need to learn anything else to make that sound. You just need to draw breath in."

If any patient in the room already has a harmonica, invite them to try it. If not, the verbal description is enough. The goal is to make the barrier feel real and low — not aspirational and distant.

Backing track off
04
3 min
Close
Slow improvised blues — quiet ending

One final piece, no preamble. Slower and more introspective than the demonstration segment. Ends with a resolved, quiet phrase. Allow the last note to decay fully before speaking. Full silence. Then the transition line.

No backing track
Spoken transition into Living Beyond SCI
Said into the silence after the closing note
"Strengthening the breath and living longer is a by-product of having a good old-fashioned blast on the harp."
One beat of silence. Then: "That's from the website. The website is called Living Beyond SCI. Let me tell you what else is on it." — and into the Guided Imagery Programme overview below.

Before each centre visit
Contact the centre in advance. Ask whether any current inpatients already have a harmonica — if so, the teach-back segment can become interactive.
Check the room acoustics. Hard floors and low ceilings affect PA placement. Err on the side of quieter rather than louder — the intimacy of the instrument is a feature, not a problem.
Run a full sound check before the audience arrives. The harmonica through a mic can sound enormous in a small room. Set levels in the empty space.
Rehearse the full 15 minutes as a single run-through, including the spoken transitions at the exact words you plan to use. The silence after the closing note must feel comfortable, not like dead air.
Load the backing track on a device that can be controlled hands-free or by a support person — you'll need to start and stop it mid-performance.
Tone note: Some people in the room may be very early in their injury. Play to the room, not at any individual. Make eye contact broadly, not pointedly.
The thread running through the visit

Breath trains the muscles. Motor imagery trains the brain. Both are things a person can do from a bed, with no equipment, with no hand function. That is the through-line connecting the harmonica performance to the Guided Imagery Programme segment that follows — and to why this visit matters clinically for the people watching. Hold it in mind during the performance and it will come through without needing to be stated explicitly until the spoken segment.

Part Two

Guided Imagery Programme
spoken overview — 2–3 minutes

Spoken casually after the performance and transition line. Not read aloud — said in your own voice, at a relaxed pace. The script below is a guide, not a script to memorise word for word.

Spoken overview

"So the harmonica grew out of something bigger that I've been working on — a website called Living Beyond SCI, which is basically a free resource for people with spinal cord injury.

One of the things on the site is a Guided Imagery Programme. It's a mind-body practice — you use mental rehearsal to imagine movement in parts of the body that may not be moving physically. There's a reasonable body of research behind it, particularly around neuroplasticity — the idea that the brain continues to respond to imagined movement even after injury.

The way I've set it up, it works with something called the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle — your brain naturally shifts into a more receptive, restful state roughly every 90 minutes. The programme is designed to sit inside that window, when the mind is already quieter and more open to this kind of practice.

Each session is an audio guide. I had them recorded using a voice clone of my own voice — which I disclose on the site, I think that's important to be upfront about.

I should be honest that it's not for everyone. There's one study that found motor imagery can occasionally increase neuropathic pain in some people, so we're clear about that on the site. It's not a treatment, it's a practice — and it's completely free.

If any of that sounds interesting, the website has everything on it — livingbeyondsci.com — and I've left a card with a QR code if you want to find it easily."

Runs approximately 2–3 minutes spoken at a relaxed pace. Leave a natural pause after the website address before inviting questions. Leave printed QR cards where patients and staff can take one.

Equipment for this visit
Harmonica rack (neck-mounted)
Selection of harmonicas — including a few spare C harps for patients to try
Portable PA — compact unit such as a Bose S1 Pro or Roland Cube Street
Dynamic microphone — Shure SM58 or similar, feedback-resistant
Short XLR cable · power board · extension cord
Printed QR cards linking to livingbeyondsci.com — leave a stack with the coordinator

Part Four

Outreach email
template — personalise before sending

Send to the Allied Health coordinator or Recreation Therapy team at each centre. Personalise the opening with the coordinator's name. The subject line is deliberately plain — it should read as an individual enquiry, not a bulk pitch.

To [Allied Health / Recreation Therapy coordinator name] From jeremy@livingbeyondsci.com Subject Visit enquiry — hands-free harmonica and guided imagery for people with SCI
Visit enquiry — hands-free harmonica and guided imagery for people with SCI

Hi [Name],

I'm writing to introduce Living Beyond SCI (livingbeyondsci.com), a free educational website I've developed for people living with spinal cord injury. The site brings together three areas: quality-of-life research, a hands-free blues harmonica course designed for people with quadriplegia, and a guided imagery programme drawing on motor imagery research.

I'm planning a trip through [city/cities] and would love the opportunity to visit your facility — either to give a short informal demonstration with patients, or simply to connect with your Allied Health or Recreation Therapy team to explore whether any of this might be useful to the people you work with.

The harmonica course uses a standard neck-mounted rack and is specifically designed for people who have limited or no hand function. The guided imagery programme is built around the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle and is freely available online with no cost to participants.

I hold a Masters of Social Sciences majoring in Psychology from Waikato University, New Zealand, and the quality-of-life research underpinning the site was conducted at Deakin University under Professor Robert Cummins. Everything on the site is free — there's nothing to sell.

I'm flexible on timing and happy to work around whatever suits your team and your patients. If it would help, I'm glad to send through more information about any of the three programmes before we commit to anything.

Would you be open to a brief conversation?
