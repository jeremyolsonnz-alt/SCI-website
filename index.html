Key Implications for Rehabilitation

Persons with SCI placed significantly greater importance than the general population on productivity, safety, and place in community — yet reported lower satisfaction in these same areas. These three domains may need greater emphasis in rehabilitation planning. Greater age and longer time since injury were both associated with higher life satisfaction, suggesting that adaptation and acceptance develop over time.

Hands-Free Programme

Harmonica for Quadriplegia

A free, web-based blues harmonica curriculum designed specifically for people with high-level spinal cord injuries — addressing both respiratory health and quality of life through music.

"Strengthening the breath and living longer is a by-product of having a good old-fashioned blast on the harp."

Jeremy Olson — Programme founder
Why the Harmonica?
Truly hands-free
One of the few instruments that can be played without any hand function, using a harmonica holder/rack. Accessible for all levels of quadriplegia.
Respiratory strengthening
Paralysis weakens diaphragm muscles, increasing risk of dangerous lung infections. Playing harmonica provides an engaging alternative to blowing through a straw.
Emotional expression
Music provides a voice for feelings that are hard to put into words — joy, grief, frustration, and hope. The blues tradition is especially suited to authentic emotional expression.
Community connection
Music creates natural social connection — jamming, performing, and sharing experience with others. Endorsed by SCI associations across Australia, Canada, NZ, South Africa, and the USA.
Free and accessible
The course requires minimal equipment — a C harmonica (~$30) and a rack holder — and is freely available online. Practice lying down, sitting, anywhere.
Quick to sound good
"The harmonica is one of the few instruments where you start sounding pretty good before long." The blues scale is just 7 notes, and songs like Spoonful can be started in the first week.

Mind-Body Therapy

Guided Imagery Exposure

A rehabilitation intervention combining naturally occurring cycles of relaxation with specific mental imagery techniques — aimed at stimulating nerve growth and improving movement and sensation after spinal cord injury.

"The imagination is one of humanity's oldest and greatest healing resources. The fact that thousands of drug studies have identified the placebo effect shows the power of the imagination."

The theoretical basis

When we think, neurons fire repeatedly throughout the brain. This releases chemicals that directly affect nerve cells of the central nervous system. The imagination is therefore not merely psychological — it is a physiological event.

Ernest Rossi's work The Psychobiology of Mind-Body Healing traces the biological pathways by which mental imagery during naturally occurring rest states can influence the immune and endocrine systems — and potentially, the nervous system itself.

GIE is designed to identify these rest windows and use them deliberately, with specific imagery directed at spinal cord regeneration. After 20 years of research, this protocol represents my best current understanding of how to direct that process.

Time commitment: ~20 minutes per day · Duration: 3 months · Outcome: unknown, dependent on injury, belief, and consistency

The five-step protocol

Each step builds on the previous. The full session runs approximately 20 minutes and is most effective during a naturally occurring rest phase.

01
Recognising the rest phase
Every 90–120 minutes, the body enters a naturally occurring cycle of relaxation — the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle (BRAC), first identified by biologists in the 1950s. These phases last anywhere from a few moments to 20 minutes. They are your window.
Signs of a rest phase: a slowing and deepening of breath, a desire to close your eyes, a sense of turning inward, a natural pause from external activity. During these states, the mind is more responsive to inner suggestions and thoughts have a greater influence on bodily processes.
Every 90–120 min 5–20 min duration Deepened breath Heightened receptivity

03
Perfect body imagery
Before sleep each night, study your chosen perfect body image until you can reproduce it clearly with eyes closed. During sessions, visualise this ideal form standing before you — in every detail, down to individual hairs, the texture of clothing, the expression of the face.
When the image is vivid, invite it to enter your body — rising up above your head, feet first, aligning itself perfectly with your own physical form. Feel your body respond. This perfect pattern is now superimposed over your nervous system. Hold the superimposed image and attempt movement of paralysed body parts. Note any new sensations, however faint. Even a light, whispy change on the skin is significant — it indicates new connections. Repeat the same movement pattern over and over to establish nervous-system memory.

04
White light breathing
A breathing exercise that combines respiratory focus with directed imagery. Imagine a sphere of bright, sparkling white light just above your head. On each inhale, draw this light down through the crown of your head and send it along your nerve pathways.
Follow the light as it travels from your brain down through the cervical spine, continuing through the thoracic and lumbar regions. When it reaches the site of injury, see it flooding the cerebrospinal fluid and bridging across the break — glowing, alive, connecting. On each exhale, release tension, doubt, and any negative thought. With each cycle, the connections grow. Ask your subconscious — that part of you that regulates heart rate, temperature, and all involuntary functions — to direct this light in whatever way will best serve healing.

Dream work and journalling
Before sleep, read the Positive Suggestions below aloud or silently. Review your day — notice where you acted from your highest ideal and where you fell short. This is not self-criticism; it is a feedback loop.
Upon waking, record your dreams immediately. The subconscious communicates through dream imagery — symbols, feelings, and narratives that can guide your waking practice. Look for feedback about your visualisation work. Over time, a two-way communication develops between your conscious intent and your unconscious process. The journal is where this dialogue becomes visible. Ask your subconscious questions before sleeping. Note any inspiration upon waking. Be consistent — this is the thread that ties the practice together across weeks and months.

Who can participate

Adults (18+) who have sustained spinal cord injury. Participation is entirely voluntary and anonymous — personal information is never linked to study data. You are free to withdraw at any time with no obligation.

Time commitment

Approximately 20 minutes per day, for three months. Sessions are most effective during naturally occurring rest phases throughout the day — not only at bedtime. Consistency matters more than duration.

What to expect

The outcome is unknown and will depend on injury level, completeness, belief systems, and consistency. Any new sensation — however faint — should be noted and reported. Awareness always precedes sensation. Progress may be subtle at first.

Important: Guided Imagery Exposure does not replace any current medical care, medications, or professional advice from your doctors. All participants take full personal responsibility for any changes that may occur. If you are interested in participating, please contact Jeremy directly.
