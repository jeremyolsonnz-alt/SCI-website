For Family and Friends

Our own research keeps saying the same thing: the people around someone with a spinal cord injury matter more than almost anything else. This page is for you — what your role actually is, and practical ways to help.

Why This Page Exists

You didn't sustain the injury, but your life changed too. That's real, and it's rarely acknowledged directly. This page exists to acknowledge it, and to tell you plainly what the research says your presence is actually worth, before it gets into anything practical.

What the Research Actually Says About Your Role

In interviews with 78 people living with spinal cord injury, one finding stood out above everything else.

The Clearest Finding in the Research

Close relationships were the most consistent source of positive coping across every single participant. Not medication, not equipment, not any particular mindset. Family members, partners, and friends were named more often than anything else as what made life liveable after SCI.

From qualitative interviews with 78 people with SCI. See the coping voices page for the complete set of responses, in their own words.

Here is some of what people actually said, unedited:

"The love of my husband, grandchildren and friends. Also my son and his first wife, with whom we are still great friends."
"But mainly the support of my wife and friends."
"My belief in God, my spirituality, and support of friends and family has allowed me to cope best with my injury. Also having a very determined mind."

If you're reading this because someone you love was recently injured, that research is about you. You are not a bystander in their recovery. You are, statistically, one of the biggest factors in how well they do.

What Actually Helps

Being named as the most important factor doesn't mean you need to have the right words, or fix anything, or manage their emotional state for them. Based on what the people in that research described, the help that mattered most was steadier and less dramatic than that.

  • Being present without rushing them toward optimism. Letting someone feel frustrated, angry, or flat without immediately trying to talk them out of it.
  • Trusting that adaptation comes on its own timeline, not on the timeline anyone else would prefer.
  • Showing up in ordinary, repeated ways rather than one big gesture. The research describes long relationships, not single rescues.
  • Letting a bit of "that's just how it went" thinking exist, rather than treating every setback as something that needed to be solved or someone's fault.

None of this requires training. It requires patience, and a willingness to stay present without an agenda.

Two Practical Ways to Help, Right Now

Beyond presence, there are a couple of specific, concrete things you can do that make a real difference, drawn directly from the two hands-on programmes on this site.

From the Harmonica Programme
Help with the neck rack setup

For someone with quadriplegia, getting the harmonica rack positioned correctly takes another pair of hands, at least for the first few sessions. The whole setup conversation is genuinely this simple: "A little higher. A little to the left. Now tighten the right wing-nut. Good." Most carers and family members get it right within two or three tries.

One trick that makes handovers between different helpers much easier: take a clear photo of the preferred setup once it's right, and keep it somewhere visible, like stuck on the wall near where practice happens. It saves re-explaining the same adjustments to whoever's helping that day.

From the Guided Imagery Programme
Read a script aloud

Each of the five guided imagery practices has a full written script available as a downloadable PDF, alongside the audio version. If someone prefers a familiar voice to a recording, or simply enjoys having company during the practice, reading the script aloud slowly, at whatever pace feels natural, is a real way to take part. There's no wrong way to do it, and no performance required.

An Honest Note

Nothing on this page suggests that supporting someone with SCI is simple, or that any of this removes the weight of what you're carrying too. The research says your presence matters enormously; it doesn't say the role is easy. If you're finding it hard, that's a normal response to something genuinely hard, not a sign you're doing it wrong.
