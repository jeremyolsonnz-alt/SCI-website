The idea

Why the harmonica?

"There must be a more interesting exercise than blowing bubbles through a straw."

— Jeremy Olson, on the standard respiratory exercise prescribed after high-level SCI

A few years ago I realised something that could change the lives of many people for the better. Paralysis after high-level spinal cord injury is often associated with weakness of the diaphragm muscles — the same muscles that drive the harmonica. This creates dangerous susceptibility to lung infections.

The harmonica is one of the few musical instruments that can be played entirely hands-free, using a neck-mounted holder. It requires no prior musical knowledge to get started. The holes are numbered. You can play lying down. And unlike blowing through a straw, it is actually enjoyable.

With the help of expert harmonica players — including Ben Hewlett — and several people with quadriplegia, I designed a web-based beginner course that has been endorsed and advertised by SCI associations in Australia, Canada, New Zealand, South Africa, and the USA.

Strengthening the breath and living longer is a by-product of having a good old-fashioned blast on the harp.

Six reasons to start

What you gain from playing

01
Respiratory strengthening
Every blow and draw works the diaphragm and respiratory muscles. Regular practice can meaningfully improve lung function and reduce infection risk for people with high-level SCI.
02
Hands-free accessibility
A harmonica rack sits around your neck and holds the instrument at your mouth. No hand function needed. You can play sitting, reclining, or lying flat — whatever your posture allows.
03
Emotional expression
The blues tradition was built on expressing what words cannot. Joy, grief, frustration, defiance, and hope all have a sound on the harp. This gives your emotional life a direct outlet.
04
Quick gratification
The harmonica is unique — you can sound good within days. The blues scale is only 7 notes. Songs like Spoonful and Happy can be started in your first week, providing early motivation.
05
Community and connection
Music naturally creates social bonds. Learning harmonica opens doors to jam sessions, online communities of players, and connection with a rich musical lineage going back to the Mississippi Delta.
06
Cognitive engagement
Learning music engages memory, attention, pattern recognition, and creative problem-solving. The self-directed practice methodology taught in this course builds metacognitive skills that transfer beyond music.
Before you start

Equipment and setup

The entire course requires two pieces of equipment. Total cost: around $30–50.

10-hole diatonic harmonica in C
Recommended: Hohner Special 20, Lee Oskar Major Diatonic, or Suzuki Bluesmaster. Buy a decent harmonica — cheap ones are harder to play, respond poorly to bending, and don't last. A C harmonica plays songs in the key of G. Low notes on the left, high on the right.
Harmonica holder / rack
Choose a rack that holds the harp securely and brings it to comfortable reach of your mouth. Place the harmonica slightly right of centre on the rack. Tighten wing-nuts so the harp cannot slip away from your lips during playing. A shirt collar or fabric pad on the chest helps stabilise the rack against your body.
Recording device (optional but recommended)
A smartphone is sufficient. Recording yourself and listening back is one of the most powerful learning tools in the course — it develops self-awareness, builds confidence, and reveals what you were unaware of while playing.
Posture tip
Make sure your posture is straight — do not reach forward in a tense position. If possible, check yourself in a mirror when setting up. Tension in the neck and shoulders will interfere with your breathing and tone.
Puckering technique
Puckering is not the same as pursing your lips for whistling. Place the harmonica well inside your mouth — enough to cover about 3 holes. Let it rest on your lower lip. Relax your jaw so the harp can move freely. Use the sides of your mouth to block the two outer holes until the centre hole sounds clean.
The Bendometer app
Once you reach Module 3 (bending), the Bendometer software at harpsoft.com is highly recommended. Play through your computer microphone and it shows you exactly which note you are hitting — invaluable visual feedback for mastering bent notes.
How to practise

The practice methodology

Good practice habits accelerate progress more than raw practice time. This course teaches you how to practise, not just what to practise.

Studying a song
Learn a song so well it enters your body. This means analysing it (taking it apart) and then synthesising it (hearing how it works as a whole). If you own it and make it yours, you can remember it. Practice gets easier when you know and love a song inside out.
  • Key (of harp and song)
  • Form (12-bars, intro, bridge)
  • Phrases — how call and response relate
  • Rhythm, melody, harmony
  • Dynamics — does it get louder or softer?
Problem-solving
Break songs into smaller, manageable chunks. Focus on the spot causing problems and master that piece. This lets you start practicing from the middle of a song without going back to the beginning every time.
  • Play slower until correct, then build speed
  • Only rehearse a piece once you have it right
  • Don't practice what you already know — that is not progress
  • If something feels easier, do more of it
Using backing tracks
Practise as much as possible with a blues rhythm track in G — piano, guitar, drums. Bookmark your favourite backing tracks on YouTube that connect you to the emotion you are trying to reach. If you can capture your mood, you can have a conversation with your listeners through the harmonica.
Recording yourself
Record and listen back. You will hear things you were unaware of during the session. Be honest: is that what you wanted to hear? If not, you now know what to work on. Recording builds confidence because you become aware of when you sound good. It also develops your ear for self-evaluation.

The course

Six progressive modules

Each module builds on the previous. Students advance at their own pace, with clear milestones at each stage.

Week 1
Foundation & Setup
Getting started right

The foundation of everything that follows. Module 1 is about producing clean, single notes and building the practice habits that will accelerate your progress throughout the course.

  • Equipment & setup — Choosing your C harmonica, fitting a holder/rack, understanding harmonica layout (low notes left, high notes right), proper posture and positioning
  • Puckering technique — Single note isolation, breathing naturally through the harmonica, finding and playing holes 1–6 accurately, first 3-hole melodies
  • Practice foundations — Creating your personalised practice schedule, the four components (Technique, Knowledge, Repertoire, Appreciation), recording yourself for self-assessment, using backing tracks from day one
Module 1 Milestone
✓Play clean single notes on holes 1–6
✓Complete a 20-minute practice session comfortably
✓Record yourself and identify one area to improve

Week 2
Rhythm & Expression
Making music with rhythm

Your first week making actual music. Chugging creates an instantly recognisable blues groove, and by the end of this module you will be improvising your first original phrases.

  • Harmonica chugging — Playing chord clusters (multiple holes), alternating blow and draw rhythmically, moving the chugging pattern across the harmonica, keeping time with backing tracks
  • First improvisation — The concept of call and response, creating 2-bar phrases using holes 1–4, playing with dynamics (loud/soft)
  • First song: Spoonful by Etta James — A simple, soulful two-note motif that demonstrates how much emotion you can express with very little
Module 2 Milestone
✓Maintain steady chugging rhythm for 12 bars
✓Create simple 2-bar improvised phrases
✓Play along with a Spoonful backing track

Weeks 3–4
Bending Notes
The essential blues sound

Bending is what makes harmonica sound like harmonica. When you scoop your tongue from the front to the back of your mouth on a draw note, you lower the pitch — creating the crying, wailing quality that defines blues music.

  • Introduction to bending — What bending is and why it matters. Physical technique: tongue position (yawning motion), air pressure, empty lungs before bending. First bends on holes 1, 2, and 3 draw
  • Draw bends across holes 1–6 — Progressive exercises, half-step vs whole-step bends, reaching bent notes cleanly without sounding the straight note first, common troubleshooting
  • Musical application — Bending for emotional effect, the "question and answer" using bent notes, classic bent note licks integrated into Spoonful
Bending tips
  • Have your lungs totally empty before starting to bend
  • You do not need to play loud — soft playing also extends harp life
  • Press your tongue firmly against your teeth, then pretend to yawn slightly
  • Use the Bendometer app to see exactly which note you are hitting

Week 5
The Blues Scale & 12-Bar Structure
The foundation of improvisation

The blues scale is only seven notes — but they can be played in a multitude of ways. Once you know it and understand the 12-bar structure, you have the keys to improvise over almost any blues song.

  • The blues scale — The seven notes: -2 -3' 4 -4'' -4 -5 6. Understanding tension and resolution in each note. Sing the scale before playing it. Visualise the pattern on the harmonica with eyes closed.
  • 12-bar structure — Standard 12-bar blues: 12 bars of 4 beats repeated. Counting bars to keep your place. The "cheat sheet" of three draw positions across a 12-bar cycle.
  • First 12-bar improvisation — Using only blues scale notes for safe improvisation, creating phrases that fit chord changes, playing with space — when not to play.
Module 4 Milestone
✓Play the blues scale from memory in both directions
✓Identify where you are in a 12-bar progression while listening
✓Improvise continuously over one complete 12-bar cycle

Weeks 6–7
Riffs, Special Effects & Harmonica Talk
Building your vocabulary

A short set of notes that sound great together is called a riff. Module 5 builds your musical vocabulary with classic riffs, expressive techniques, and the unique ability to make your harmonica "speak" using speech consonants.

  • Classic riffs — Good Morning, Up, Trouble, Mannish Boy, Duke, Low-Down Blues. When and how to use each. Creating variations.
  • Special effects — Slides (up to and down from notes), throat vibrato ("ha-ha-ha" on airflow), trills (head movement side to side), octaves (holes 1+4, 2+5 etc. with tongue blocking middle holes)
  • Harmonica talk — Using English pronunciation strategies to phrase notes. Consonants: b (bed), d (dog), t (tip), k (cat), r (rug). Vowels: a, e, i, o, u — long and short. This makes the harmonica literally speak.

Weeks 8+
Performance & Advanced Improvisation
Becoming performance-ready

The final module is about putting it all together — building longer musical statements, playing with other musicians, and developing your own distinctive voice. This is where technique becomes artistry.

  • Advanced improvisation — Building 4–8 bar phrases, dramatic arcs (tension and release), targeting chord tones for stronger resolution, trading fours with a backing track
  • Playing with others — Musical etiquette: when to play, when to listen. Never play over a singer or lead instrument. Play between vocal lines. Adjusting volume and tone to fit the band. Choosing the right harmonica key for different songs.
  • Your performance repertoire — Refining all four songs (Spoonful, Happy, Roadhouse Blues, Smells Like Teen Spirit) plus one student-choice song. Recording a performance-quality video.
  • Continuing your journey — Finding local jam sessions and open mic nights, exploring different keys and positions, next steps: amplified harmonica, chromatic harmonica, advanced techniques
