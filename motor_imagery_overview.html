Pillar 3 — Research Overview

The Science of Imagining Movement

Motor imagery is the practice of rehearsing a movement in your mind, without moving. This is a plain-language tour of what the research actually shows, in spinal cord injury, and in three other conditions where the same idea has been tested: stroke, Parkinson's disease, and multiple sclerosis.

Close your eyes and picture yourself picking up a cup of tea. Feel your arm reach out, your fingers close around the handle, the small lift as you raise it toward your mouth. You did not move. But if you were lying in a brain scanner while you did that, parts of your brain would have lit up as though you had.

That simple fact (that imagining a movement switches on much of the same brain machinery as actually doing it) is the foundation of everything on this page. Scientists call the practice motor imagery. In everyday words, it is the mental rehearsal of movement. Athletes have used it for a century. A high jumper stands at the end of the runway, eyes shut, running the whole jump in their head before they take a single step. What is newer, and what this page is about, is the growing body of research asking whether the same trick can help people whose movement has been taken away or disrupted by injury or illness.

This overview walks through that research in ordinary language. You do not need a science background to follow it. Where a technical term is useful, it is explained the first time it appears. The aim is not to sell you on motor imagery. It is to lay out honestly what has been found, how strong the evidence is, and, just as importantly, where the evidence is thin, mixed, or points the other way.

We begin with the brain science that makes the whole idea plausible. Then we look closely at the condition this website exists to serve: spinal cord injury. After that, we widen the lens to three other neurological conditions where motor imagery has been studied more, and sometimes more rigorously: stroke, Parkinson's disease, and multiple sclerosis. Seeing the picture across all four conditions tells us more than any one of them could alone. It shows where motor imagery reliably helps, where it does not, and what those patterns mean for someone deciding whether to try it.

Why does this matter enough to warrant a long, careful read? Because rehabilitation after a serious neurological event is often a story of scarcity. Therapy time is limited and expensive. Progress can be slow and uncertain. Many of the most promising-sounding treatments turn out, on inspection, to rest on hope more than evidence. Against that backdrop, a technique that costs nothing, requires no equipment, carries little risk for most people, and can be practised alone at home is worth taking seriously, but only if it actually does something. The whole purpose of a page like this is to help you tell the difference between a genuine, evidence-backed tool and wishful thinking dressed in scientific language. Motor imagery, as we will see, is mostly the former, with honest edges of uncertainty that this page will not paper over.

Part One — The Foundations

How imagining movement works in the brain

To understand why researchers think motor imagery might help after a neurological injury, it helps to understand one central idea. It has a slightly clumsy name (the functional equivalence hypothesis) but the concept behind it is simple and rather beautiful.

The idea is this: when you vividly imagine a movement, your brain does not treat it as pure daydreaming. It treats it as a kind of quiet, held-back version of the real thing. The same regions that plan and command movement become active. The main difference is that, at the last moment, the command to actually move is blocked before it reaches your muscles. You get the full mental rehearsal without the physical action.

This idea was set out most influentially by a French neuroscientist named Marc Jeannerod in the 1990s. He proposed that a motor image (a mentally rehearsed movement) carries the same properties as the real motor plan it stands for, and therefore engages the brain's motor system in much the same way (Jeannerod, 1994). Over the following three decades, brain-imaging technology caught up with the theory and largely confirmed it. When people lie in scanners and imagine movements, researchers consistently see activity in the same network of motor-planning areas that fire during real movement: the premotor cortex, the supplementary motor area, and related regions (Hardwick et al., 2018).

Imagining a movement and making a movement are not two completely different activities. They are more like a full performance and a silent dress rehearsal using the same stage and the same actors.

Why would that matter for rehabilitation? Because the brain is not a fixed machine. It changes in response to what we ask it to do: a property called neuroplasticity. Every skill you have ever learned, from tying a shoelace to playing an instrument, was laid down by repeatedly using and strengthening particular pathways in the brain. Practice does not just train the muscles; it trains the brain's command structure. Studies of imagined practice have shown that mental rehearsal alone can produce measurable improvements in strength and skill, driven not by changes in the muscles but by more efficient signals coming down from the motor cortex (Hardwick et al., 2018).

This is the crucial link. If the barrier to movement is not the muscle itself but a disrupted connection between brain and body (as it is in many neurological conditions) then keeping the brain's motor system active and well-organised may be valuable in its own right. Motor imagery offers a way to exercise that system when ordinary movement is difficult or impossible.

Two ways to imagine

Not all imagining is the same, and the difference turns out to matter a great deal for whether it works.

The first kind is visual imagery: you picture yourself moving, as though watching a film of your own body. The second is kinaesthetic imagery: you imagine the feeling of the movement from the inside: the effort, the stretch, the weight, the sense of your limb travelling through space. Both are useful, but the research points repeatedly to kinaesthetic imagery (the felt, from-the-inside kind) as the more powerful for rehabilitation, because it engages the motor system more fully than simply watching a mental picture (Ladda et al., 2021).

A second lesson from the research is about timing. Imagined movements that unfold at roughly the same speed as the real movement engage the brain more faithfully than imagined movements that are rushed or slowed. A large review of best practice found that motor imagery works best when it is vivid, kinaesthetic rather than merely visual, and matched in time to the natural duration of the real movement (Ladda et al., 2021). These are not fussy details. They are the difference between imagery that helps and imagery that does little at all.

The core idea in one paragraph

Imagining a movement activates much of the same brain machinery as performing it. Because the brain changes in response to use, keeping the motor system active through imagery may help maintain or rebuild it, especially when ordinary movement is hard. Imagery works best when it is felt from the inside, vivid, and matched to the real movement's speed.

How to read the evidence that follows

Before we look at the studies, a few words about how to weigh them, because not all evidence carries the same weight and it is easy to be misled by a confident-sounding claim built on shaky ground.

The gold standard in this kind of research is the randomised controlled trial. In such a trial, participants are randomly sorted into a group that receives the treatment and a group that does not, and the two are compared. Randomising is what makes it fair: it means the two groups should be similar in every way except the treatment, so any difference in outcome can more confidently be pinned on the treatment itself rather than on some hidden difference between the people. The very best of these trials also give the comparison group a convincing sham activity, so that neither the extra attention nor the simple act of resting can masquerade as a real effect.

Above single trials sit systematic reviews and meta-analyses. A systematic review gathers every study that meets a careful set of standards and summarises them together. A meta-analysis goes a step further and statistically combines their results into a single overall estimate. When many separate trials point the same way and a meta-analysis confirms it, the conclusion is far more trustworthy than any single study. But there is a catch that appears repeatedly in the motor imagery literature: when the individual studies are very different from one another (different patients, different imagery methods, different ways of measuring success) they cannot be cleanly combined, and reviewers are forced to describe the results in words rather than distil them into one number. You will see this caution stated again and again below. It is not a reason to dismiss the evidence, but it is a reason to hold it with an open hand rather than a clenched fist.

Finally, a healthy respect for negative results (trials that found no benefit) is essential. There is a natural temptation, in science as in life, to notice the successes and forget the failures. A trustworthy summary does the opposite: it holds the encouraging and the disappointing findings in view at the same time. This page tries to do that throughout, and it is why, in the sections that follow, you will find null results reported alongside the positive ones rather than quietly left out.

A century of quiet evidence

Although the brain-scanning proof is recent, the practical observation is old. As far back as the 1930s, researchers noticed something curious: people who merely imagined practising a physical skill (throwing darts, say, or tossing rings onto a peg) often improved almost as much as people who physically practised, and far more than people who did nothing at all. For decades this sat in the world of sports psychology as an intriguing curiosity. Athletes and coaches adopted it long before anyone could explain it. Divers rehearsed their twists on the board. Gymnasts ran through routines with their eyes closed. It worked often enough that it became part of the culture of elite sport, an accepted piece of the mental toolkit.

What changed in the last thirty years is that the explanation caught up with the practice. Once it became possible to watch the living brain at work, researchers could finally see what had been happening all along: the imagining athlete's motor system was genuinely engaged, quietly rehearsing the movement it would later perform. And once the mechanism was understood, a natural question followed. If mental rehearsal could sharpen a healthy athlete's skill by exercising their motor system, could it help maintain or rebuild the motor system of someone whose movement had been disrupted by injury or disease? That question is what moved motor imagery out of the gym and into the rehabilitation clinic, and it is what the rest of this page is about.

What actually happens in a session

It is worth being concrete about what motor imagery looks like in practice, because the word "imagery" can sound vaguer and more mystical than the reality. A typical session is quiet and structured. A person settles into a comfortable position, often with eyes closed. They are guided (by a therapist, a recording, or their own memory of instructions) to imagine a specific movement in detail. Not a symbol of movement, not a wish to move, but a particular action: reaching for a cup, taking a step, closing the hand around a door handle. They are encouraged to feel it from the inside, to sense the effort and the weight and the travel of the limb, and to let the imagined movement take about as long as the real one would.

Sessions are usually short (often ten to twenty minutes) and repeated several times a week, because like any form of practice, motor imagery depends on repetition to build and hold its effect. There is nothing exotic about the setup. That plainness is part of the appeal: it needs no equipment, no clinic, and no physical exertion, which makes it one of the most accessible rehabilitation tools that exists. A person can do it in a hospital bed, in a wheelchair, or lying at home in the dark.

With that foundation in place, we can turn to the conditions themselves, starting with spinal cord injury.

Part Two — Spinal Cord Injury

Motor imagery after spinal cord injury

A spinal cord injury does something specific and cruel. In most cases, the brain is completely intact. The muscles, too, are often perfectly healthy. What is damaged is the cable between them: the spinal cord, which carries the signals from brain to body. The command to move is still generated in the brain. It simply cannot get through.

This is exactly the situation where motor imagery becomes interesting. Because the brain's motor system is usually undamaged after a spinal cord injury, the machinery that motor imagery exercises is still there. The question researchers have asked is whether keeping that machinery active (through imagined movement) can do anything useful for people whose physical movement is limited or gone.

What happens to the brain after the injury

When a part of the body can no longer be moved or felt, the brain does not simply hold that region in reserve, waiting. Over time, the territory in the motor cortex devoted to the affected limbs can begin to shrink and reorganise, as neighbouring functions gradually encroach on the now-quiet area. This is the brain's efficiency at work, but for someone hoping to preserve or recover function, it is not necessarily welcome. One rationale for motor imagery is that by continuing to "use" the affected limbs in the mind, a person may help hold that cortical territory open and keep the motor maps from fading (Di Rienzo et al., 2016).

There is an important and honest complication here, though. The ability to perform motor imagery is not always perfectly preserved after spinal cord injury. Research suggests that long-term loss of movement and sensation can, in some people, subtly change how well they can imagine movement in the affected limbs, particularly the automatic, unconscious form of imagery the brain uses to judge body position. The findings on this are mixed and still being worked out (Scandola et al., 2024). In plain terms: most people with spinal cord injury can imagine movement well, but it is not safe to assume the ability is identical to before the injury, and this is one reason imagery ability is sometimes assessed before training begins.

What the clinical studies have found

The strongest evidence for motor imagery in spinal cord injury concerns the hands and arms: specifically, reaching and grasping in people with tetraplegia, where injury high on the spine affects all four limbs. A review of ten studies in this area found that mentally rehearsing movements that were still partly possible improved reach-and-grasp performance in several concrete ways: people showed increased grip capability and muscle strength, they moved more quickly and with less wobble in their movement path, and, strikingly, brain scans showed that the abnormally high, effortful brain activity that often accompanies movement after injury actually calmed down, becoming more efficient (Mateo et al., 2015). That last point matters. It suggests the imagery was not just improving performance on the surface but reshaping how the brain organised the movement underneath.

Why the hands, above all else? Because for many people living with tetraplegia, the return of even a little hand function would matter more than almost anything else, more than walking, in survey after survey. The ability to hold a fork, manage a phone, or grip a rail is the difference between depending on others and doing something for yourself. Research into the priorities of people with tetraplegia has consistently found hand and arm function at or near the top of the list. So the fact that motor imagery's clearest spinal cord injury evidence sits precisely in reach-and-grasp is not a minor technicality. It lands on exactly the function people most want back.

Beyond the hands, motor imagery has been tested for walking-related function and for daily independence, usually as an add-on to conventional physiotherapy rather than a replacement for it. A broad systematic review that gathered together 18 studies involving 282 patients found that, in most of them, motor imagery was associated with improved motor function and reduced pain, though the review was careful to note that the studies varied so much in design that the results could only be described, not pooled into a single tidy number (Opsommer et al., 2020). This is a recurring theme in this field, and an honest one: the direction of the evidence is encouraging, but the studies are often small and hard to compare.

It is worth pausing on why the evidence is thinner here than in stroke, because the reason is not that motor imagery works less well after spinal cord injury. It is that spinal cord injury is, thankfully, far less common than stroke, which means fewer patients, fewer trials, and smaller studies. Rehabilitation research follows the numbers. A condition that affects millions attracts large, well-funded trials; a condition that affects far fewer people is studied in smaller, scrappier ways. When judging the spinal cord injury evidence, then, it helps to read it alongside the much larger stroke literature, because the underlying mechanism (exercising an intact or partly intact motor system through imagery) is shared between them. What works in the brain after a stroke is not a different biological process from what works in the brain after a spinal cord injury. The evidence in one lends cautious support to the plausibility of the other.

The command to move is still generated in the brain. It simply cannot get through. Motor imagery exercises the part of the system that the injury usually leaves intact.

The pain warning: stated plainly

Here is the finding this programme takes most seriously, and refuses to hide. For most people, imagining movement is comfortable and calming. But a well-known study found that in some people with neuropathic pain following a complete spinal cord injury, imagining movement actually increased their pain (Gustin et al., 2008). Neuropathic pain is the burning, electrical, or shooting pain that comes from the injured nervous system itself, rather than from any damage in the painful area. For a subset of people, stirring up the motor system through imagery appears to stir up this pain as well.

This does not mean motor imagery is dangerous, and it does not mean anyone should be frightened away from trying it. What it means is that motor imagery is not automatically harmless for everyone, and that the sensible approach is to pay attention to your own body. If imagining movement reliably increases your pain, that is a clear signal to stop and to talk it through with your rehabilitation team. A systematic review that looked specifically at both the benefits and the risks concluded that motor imagery shows promise for both motor function and, in most people, for pain, while acknowledging that in a minority it can do the opposite (Opsommer et al., 2020). Honesty about that minority is not a weakness in the case for motor imagery. It is what makes the rest of the case trustworthy.

What this means for the Guided Imagery Programme

The programme on this site is built on this evidence, and on its limits. Motor imagery is offered as a calm, low-cost practice that engages a part of the nervous system the injury usually leaves intact. It is presented as a serious complement to physical rehabilitation, never a replacement for it, and never a promise of recovery.

The pain finding above is the reason the programme asks you to monitor your own experience and to stop if imagining movement consistently increases your pain. Outcomes vary between individuals. The evidence is genuinely promising, and it is genuinely still growing. Both of those things are true at once.

A closer look at the brain-map argument

One of the most compelling reasons to take motor imagery seriously after spinal cord injury has to do with what brain scans reveal about the injured nervous system over time. In the healthy brain, each part of the body has a corresponding territory in the motor cortex: a kind of map, where the hand, the arm, the leg, and so on each occupy their own patch. After a spinal cord injury cuts off a limb from the brain's commands, that limb's patch on the map does not simply wait patiently. Deprived of its usual traffic, it can begin to blur and be encroached upon by neighbouring territories. Researchers have documented these shifts in cortical organisation following spinal cord injury (Di Rienzo et al., 2016).

This matters for a hopeful reason and a cautionary one. The hopeful reason is that motor imagery may help preserve those maps. By continuing to send planning signals toward the affected limbs (even signals that cannot complete their journey down the spinal cord) a person may keep the corresponding brain territory active and better organised. If a future treatment or a degree of natural recovery ever reopens some of the connection between brain and body, a better-preserved map might mean a better foundation to build on. This is a reasonable hope grounded in real observations, though it is important to be clear that it remains a hope rather than a proven outcome.

The cautionary reason is the flip side. The same reorganisation that motor imagery might help resist can also, in some people, affect the imagery itself. The brain that is remapping is the brain being asked to imagine; as noted above, this is part of why imagery ability after spinal cord injury is not perfectly uniform from person to person. None of this undoes the case for trying motor imagery. But it does explain why researchers approach it as something to be assessed and tailored rather than applied identically to everyone, and why the honest framing throughout this programme is one of grounded possibility rather than promise.

To understand how much weight this evidence can bear, it helps to step outside spinal cord injury and look at conditions where motor imagery has been studied for longer and in larger numbers. The clearest of these, by a wide margin, is stroke.

Part Three — Stroke

Stroke: the largest body of evidence

If you want to know how well a rehabilitation idea really works, you look for the condition where it has been tested the most. For motor imagery, that condition is stroke. There are more studies, more patients, and more high-quality trials in stroke than in any other area, which makes it the natural place to judge what motor imagery can and cannot do.

A stroke happens when the blood supply to part of the brain is cut off, usually by a clot or a bleed, and brain tissue is damaged as a result. Unlike spinal cord injury, where the brain is typically intact and the cable is cut, a stroke damages the brain itself. Often this leaves one side of the body weak or paralysed: an arm that will not lift, a hand that will not open, a leg that drags. Because the damage is in the brain, and because the brain is capable of reorganising itself, stroke rehabilitation is fundamentally about coaxing the surviving parts of the brain to take over what the damaged parts can no longer do. Motor imagery is one tool for that coaxing.

What the trials show

Before looking at the trials, it helps to understand why timing matters so much in stroke. In the weeks and months after a stroke, the brain enters a period of heightened plasticity: a window during which it is unusually ready to rewire itself and adapt. Rehabilitation aims to make the most of this window, cramming in as much useful practice as possible while the brain is most receptive. But there is a problem: in the early days after a stroke, a person is often too weak, too tired, or too impaired to do much physical practice. Their arm may not move at all. This is precisely where motor imagery offers something valuable. When the body cannot yet practise, the mind still can. Motor imagery lets a person begin rehearsing movement during the very window when the brain is most ready to change, even before the muscles will cooperate.

The idea of using mental rehearsal after stroke goes back decades, but the serious clinical testing has largely happened since the 2000s. One influential randomised controlled trial (the kind of study where patients are randomly assigned either to receive the treatment or not, which is the fairest way to test whether something truly works) looked at stroke patients who still had lasting weakness in one arm. Those who added mental practice of movement to their normal therapy improved more than those who did not (Page et al., 2007). It was one of the first solid signals that imagining movement could translate into real gains in the clinic, not just in theory. What made the trial especially persuasive was its design: the comparison group did a sham activity of the same length, so the benefit could not simply be explained by the extra attention or the relaxation of sitting quietly. Something about the imagining itself made the difference.

Since then, the evidence has been gathered and re-gathered many times. A systematic review and meta-analysis (a study that statistically combines the results of many separate trials) pulled together 32 studies of motor imagery after stroke. It concluded that imagery training had value for improving motor function, while noting the same challenge that runs through this whole field: the studies used a wide variety of methods, which makes it harder to state a single clean result (Kho et al., 2014). A more recent meta-analysis of ten carefully selected randomised trials found that practising motor imagery, even just a couple of times a week over a few weeks, improved the movement performance of stroke patients when it was added on top of their usual rehabilitation (Monteiro et al., 2021).

A key pattern worth noticing

Across the stroke research, motor imagery works best as a complement to physical therapy, not a substitute for it. The gains tend to appear when imagery is added on top of real practice: the mental rehearsal and the physical rehearsal reinforcing each other. This add-on pattern shows up again and again across every condition on this page.

The technology twist: brain-computer interfaces

One of the most interesting recent developments combines motor imagery with technology. A brain-computer interface is a system that reads brain activity directly (usually through sensors on the scalp) and translates it into a signal a computer can use. When a stroke patient imagines moving their weak hand, the interface detects the motor-planning activity and, for example, drives a device that helps the hand move, or moves an object on a screen. The person imagines; the machine responds; the loop closes.

The appeal is that this gives immediate feedback. Ordinarily, imagining a movement produces nothing you can see, which makes it hard to know whether you are doing it well. A brain-computer interface turns the invisible act of imagining into a visible result, which both confirms the effort and may strengthen the underlying brain activity. A meta-analysis of eight randomised trials involving 357 stroke patients found that this combined approach produced a clear, moderate-to-large improvement in patients' ability to carry out everyday activities, alongside a promising but less statistically certain effect on arm movement itself (Zhang et al., 2025). Neuroimaging in these studies suggested the training was strengthening activity in the injured half of the brain and helping the motor areas reorganise, exactly the kind of rewiring stroke rehabilitation is trying to achieve.

Keeping it in proportion

Stroke has the strongest evidence base for motor imagery, but even here the researchers are careful. The most honest summary is that motor imagery is an effective complement to conventional rehabilitation: helpful when added to real therapy, supported by many trials, but not a stand-alone cure and not equally effective for everyone. Reviewers consistently call for more standardised, higher-quality studies. That the strongest field still says "promising, add-on, needs better trials" is itself useful information when weighing the thinner evidence in other conditions.

Stroke shows motor imagery at its most tested. Parkinson's disease shows something different and instructive: a condition where the problem is not weakness but the planning and initiating of movement itself, and where the evidence is genuinely mixed.

Part Four — Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease: movement and its planning

Parkinson's disease is different in kind from the two conditions we have looked at so far. It is not an injury that happens in a moment, but a gradual condition in which certain brain cells (the ones that produce a chemical messenger called dopamine) slowly stop working. Dopamine is essential for smooth, automatic movement, and as it dwindles, movement becomes slow, stiff, and harder to start. People with Parkinson's often describe knowing exactly what they want to do but being unable to get their body to begin.

That description is a clue to why motor imagery is interesting here. The problem in Parkinson's is heavily tied up with the planning and initiation of movement: the very stages of movement that motor imagery engages. In theory, rehearsing movements in the mind might help prime the brain's movement-planning circuits and make real movements easier to launch. One symptom in particular has drawn attention: freezing of gait, the sudden, frightening sensation of the feet becoming glued to the floor mid-walk, which is a major cause of falls.

To see why the theory is appealing, it helps to know a little about what dopamine does. In a healthy brain, dopamine helps make well-practised movements automatic: you do not consciously plan how to walk across a room, you simply do it, and a deep brain structure called the basal ganglia handles the details below the level of awareness. In Parkinson's, as dopamine fades, that automatic system falters. Movements that were once effortless now require conscious attention and deliberate effort. Interestingly, people with Parkinson's can often move much better when they consciously focus on the movement, or when something external (a line on the floor, a beat, a spoken cue) gives their brain a target to aim at. Motor imagery fits naturally into this picture: it is, in essence, a way of consciously and deliberately engaging the movement-planning system that the disease has made less automatic. That is the hope, at least. Whether it delivers is where the evidence becomes complicated.

Where the evidence gets genuinely mixed

This is the section where honesty matters most, because Parkinson's is where motor imagery's results are least consistent, and pretending otherwise would misrepresent the science.

On the encouraging side, several reviews have found that motor imagery and the closely related practice of action observation (watching someone else perform a movement, which activates similar brain circuits) show promise for improving motor symptoms in Parkinson's. A systematic review focused on these mirror-based approaches concluded that they can help facilitate motor relearning and may benefit gait (Singer et al., 2024). Some individual trials have reported improvements in walking and in the ability to manage two tasks at once, sometimes accompanied by visible changes on brain scans.

But the picture is far from uniform. One well-conducted randomised controlled trial set out specifically to test whether action observation and motor imagery could reduce freezing of gait and improve balance, and found no effect on either (Bezerra et al., 2022). This is not a result to explain away. It is a direct, honest finding from a fair test, and it sits alongside the more positive studies rather than being cancelled out by them. When the same review that reports promise also reports null results like this, the correct conclusion is not "it works" or "it doesn't work" but something more careful: it may help some people with some symptoms, and it clearly does not help everyone with everything.

When a fair test finds no effect, that result deserves the same respect as the positive ones. Reporting only the encouraging findings would not be a summary of the science. It would be advertising.

A further complication specific to Parkinson's is that the disease can itself affect the ability to perform motor imagery. Because the condition disrupts the same movement-planning circuits that imagery relies on, some people with Parkinson's find their imagery becomes less vivid as the disease progresses, and the vividness of their imagery has been linked to the severity of their symptoms (Readman et al., 2023). This creates a genuine puzzle: the tool depends on a capacity that the disease may erode. It does not make motor imagery useless in Parkinson's, but it does help explain why the results are so variable from person to person and study to study.

The rhythm connection

One consistent thread in the more positive Parkinson's research is the pairing of imagery with rhythm: for example, imagining walking in time to a steady beat. Rhythmic cues have a well-documented ability to help people with Parkinson's move more smoothly, seemingly by giving the brain an external timekeeper to lean on when its own internal timing falters. When motor imagery is combined with this kind of rhythmic cueing, the results tend to be more encouraging than imagery alone. This same rhythm-and-imagery combination, as we are about to see, is exactly what has shown the clearest promise in multiple sclerosis.

The Parkinson's takeaway

Motor imagery in Parkinson's is a case of real but inconsistent evidence. Some trials show benefit for gait and motor symptoms, especially when imagery is paired with rhythmic cues; at least one rigorous trial found no effect on freezing of gait or balance. The disease can also blunt the imagery ability that the technique depends on. The fair summary: worth exploring as part of a broader programme, with realistic expectations and no guarantee of benefit.

Part Five — Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis: fatigue and walking

Multiple sclerosis, usually shortened to MS, is a condition in which the body's own immune system attacks the protective coating around nerve fibres in the brain and spinal cord. This coating, called myelin, works like the insulation around an electrical wire: it lets signals travel quickly and cleanly. When it is damaged, the signals slow down, scatter, or fail to arrive. The effects are wide-ranging and vary enormously from person to person, but two of the most common and most life-limiting are difficulty walking and a profound, heavy fatigue that ordinary rest does not fix.

Motor imagery has been studied in MS with a particular focus on these two problems, and, interestingly, the MS research has produced some of the more clearly positive results in this whole overview, with a distinctive twist that ties the field together.

There is a reason to think the brain-training logic of motor imagery might apply well in MS. Although MS damages the insulation around nerve fibres, the brain retains a considerable capacity to adapt and find new routes for its signals: to reroute around the damage, much as traffic finds a way around a blocked road. Rehabilitation in MS leans on this adaptability. Anything that keeps the motor system active and well-practised may help the brain make the most of the pathways it still has. Motor imagery, by exercising the movement-planning system without demanding physical exertion, offers a way to do exactly that. And because the damage in MS is scattered and variable rather than concentrated in one place, a gentle, whole-system form of mental practice has a certain logic to it.

Walking, fatigue, and quality of life

A systematic review that gathered together the studies of motor imagery in MS found a consistent and encouraging pattern: people with MS who practised motor imagery showed improvements in how fast and how far they could walk, in their levels of fatigue, and in their overall quality of life, with additional benefits seen in balance and in their confidence about walking (Seebacher et al., 2023). For a condition where fatigue and walking difficulty can quietly shrink a person's world, improvements in exactly those areas are meaningful.

The distinctive twist is rhythm. The most striking MS studies did not use motor imagery in silence. They paired it with a steady beat (music or a metronome) so that people imagined themselves walking in time to a rhythm. In one carefully designed randomised controlled trial, this rhythmic-cued motor imagery improved walking, reduced fatigue, and lifted quality of life in people with MS (Seebacher et al., 2017). The researchers suggested that the rhythm and the imagery worked together, the beat giving the imagined movement a scaffold to hang on. This echoes exactly what we saw in the more successful Parkinson's studies, and it is one of the clearest cross-condition lessons in the entire body of research: imagery paired with rhythm often outperforms imagery alone.

A thread that runs through everything

In MS and in Parkinson's, the strongest results came when motor imagery was combined with a steady rhythm: imagining movement in time to a beat. Rhythm appears to give the brain an external timekeeper that strengthens the mental rehearsal. It is a small, practical detail with real consequences, and it is one of the most transferable findings across conditions.

Why MS suits motor imagery

There is a practical reason motor imagery fits MS particularly well, and it comes back to fatigue. Traditional exercise-based rehabilitation can be hard for people with MS to tolerate, precisely because exertion worsens the fatigue and can temporarily intensify symptoms. Motor imagery sidesteps this. It is mentally engaging but physically gentle: you can rehearse a demanding movement without the physical cost of performing it repeatedly. For someone whose energy is rationed by their condition, a form of practice that trains the movement system without draining the body has an obvious appeal. Reviewers have highlighted exactly this: that motor imagery, especially with rhythmic cues, may be a well-tolerated way to work on walking and fatigue when conventional exertion is not an option (Seebacher et al., 2023).

The MS takeaway

The MS evidence is among the more encouraging in this overview, particularly for walking, fatigue, and quality of life, and especially when imagery is paired with rhythm. As always, the studies are relatively small and call for larger trials. But the fit between what motor imagery offers (gentle, non-fatiguing mental practice) and what MS demands (working around fatigue) is a genuinely good one.

Part Six — Drawing It Together

What the four conditions tell us together

Looking at spinal cord injury, stroke, Parkinson's disease, and multiple sclerosis side by side reveals patterns that no single condition could show on its own. Four lessons stand out.

1. Motor imagery is a complement, not a cure

In every condition, the clearest benefits appeared when imagery was added to physical rehabilitation, not used in place of it. The mental rehearsal and the physical practice reinforce each other. Nowhere in this literature does motor imagery replace real therapy or promise recovery on its own. Any honest account has to lead with this, and this programme does.

2. The evidence is promising but uneven

Stroke has the deepest evidence, with many trials pointing the same encouraging direction. MS shows clear promise for walking and fatigue. Spinal cord injury shows real benefit for hand and arm function, on a smaller evidence base. Parkinson's is genuinely mixed, with strong positive studies sitting alongside fair trials that found nothing. This unevenness is not a flaw in the summary: it is the actual state of the science, and it should shape expectations accordingly.

3. How you imagine matters

The same practical rules improved results across conditions: imagery felt from the inside (kinaesthetic) beats imagery merely watched; vivid beats vague; and imagined movement matched to the real movement's speed beats rushed rehearsal. And repeatedly (in MS and Parkinson's especially) pairing imagery with a steady rhythm outperformed imagery alone. These are not incidental details. They are the difference between practice that helps and practice that does little.

4. The tool depends on a capacity the condition can affect

Motor imagery relies on the brain's ability to generate a vivid mental movement, and several of these conditions can quietly erode that ability. Long-term spinal cord injury may alter it; Parkinson's may blunt it as the disease advances. This is why imagery ability is sometimes assessed before training, and why results vary so much from one person to the next. It is also a reminder that motor imagery is a skill, not a switch: something that can be practised, strengthened, and, in some people, found difficult.

Seen across four conditions, motor imagery looks less like a miracle and more like what it actually is: a real, useful, well-grounded technique with clear limits, worth doing, worth doing well, and worth honest expectations.

A note on why comparing conditions is fair

A reasonable person might object at this point: these four conditions are so different (a severed spinal cord, a damaged brain, a chemical shortage, a scattered loss of insulation) that lumping their evidence together seems like comparing apples with oranges. It is a fair worry, and it deserves an answer.

The answer is that motor imagery does not act on the injury. It acts on the motor system (the brain's machinery for planning and commanding movement) and that machinery is broadly the same in everyone. What differs between the conditions is the nature of the obstacle standing between that machinery and actual movement. In spinal cord injury, the obstacle is a cut cable. In stroke, it is damage to the machinery itself. In Parkinson's, it is a chemical shortage that makes the machinery sluggish. In MS, it is faulty insulation slowing the signals. Motor imagery reaches back to the shared machinery, upstream of all these different obstacles, and exercises it. That is why it is legitimate to learn from all four at once: they are four different windows onto the same underlying question of whether exercising the motor system through imagination produces real-world benefit. The conditions differ; the tool, and the part of the brain it works on, are shared.

Who seems to benefit most

Reading across the evidence, a rough picture emerges of where motor imagery has the most to offer. It appears most valuable when a person's motor-planning system is relatively intact but cannot easily drive physical movement, as after a spinal cord injury, or in the early days after a stroke when the arm will not yet cooperate. It appears well-suited to people for whom ordinary exercise is exhausting or poorly tolerated, as in MS, because it trains movement without the physical cost. And it appears to work best in people who can generate vivid, felt mental images, a capacity that varies between individuals and can be reduced by some of these very conditions. None of this is a strict rule, and motor imagery is cheap and safe enough that it is usually reasonable to try. But it does help explain why the same technique produces strong results in one study and none in another: the fit between the tool and the person is not always the same.

Part Seven — In Practice

What good practice looks like

If the research points to anything actionable, it is that how motor imagery is done shapes whether it helps. Pulling the threads together, a few principles emerge that apply regardless of the underlying condition.

Imagine from the inside. Rather than watching yourself as if on a screen, try to feel the movement: the weight, the effort, the stretch, the sensation of your limb moving through space. This kinaesthetic quality is the single most consistent marker of effective imagery in the research (Ladda et al., 2021).

Keep it vivid and specific. A detailed, richly imagined movement engages the motor system more than a vague one. Picture a real, concrete action in a real setting, not an abstract idea of movement.

Match the real speed. Imagine the movement unfolding at the pace it would actually happen. Rushing through it in your mind, or dragging it out, weakens the link between the imagined and the real.

Consider adding rhythm. Imagining movement in time to a steady beat (music or a metronome) strengthened results in several studies, particularly for walking. This is one of the most transferable findings in the whole field.

Pair it with real practice where you can. Motor imagery consistently works best as a companion to physical rehabilitation. Where any movement is possible, imagery and physical practice together tend to beat either one alone.

Watch your own response, especially pain. For most people, motor imagery is calming. But if imagining movement reliably increases your pain, that is a real signal, not something to push through. Stop, and raise it with your rehabilitation team (Gustin et al., 2008; Opsommer et al., 2020).

Building the skill over time

It helps to treat motor imagery as a skill that improves with practice rather than an ability you either have or lack. Many people find their first attempts frustrating: the mental picture is dim, the felt sensation faint, the attention wandering. This is normal and it improves. A common way to begin is with very simple movements: opening and closing a hand, bending an elbow, tapping a foot. Once these feel vivid and controlled in the mind, more complex or more personally meaningful movements can be built up from them.

Some people find their imagery is strongly visual (they see the movement clearly but struggle to feel it) while others feel it easily but see little. Both starting points are workable. The aim over time is to strengthen the felt, kinaesthetic side, since that is the more powerful ingredient, but there is no need to force it all at once. Warming up with a real movement first, where any movement is possible, can make the subsequent imagined version more vivid, because the body has just supplied the brain with a fresh template to rehearse.

Consistency tends to matter more than intensity. Short sessions done regularly (a few times a week, over weeks) reflect how the successful studies were actually run, and they suit the reality of living with a neurological condition better than occasional marathon efforts. Fatigue is real, attention is finite, and a brief, focused, vivid rehearsal is worth more than a long, foggy one. Quality of imagining beats quantity.

When it is not working

If motor imagery feels like nothing is happening, a few adjustments often help. Simplify the movement until it becomes vivid again. Slow down and add more sensory detail: the temperature, the texture, the weight, the sound. Try imagining from inside your own body rather than watching yourself from outside. Introduce a steady rhythm to move in time with. And if, after honest effort over some weeks, imagery remains genuinely difficult or produces no sense of engagement, that is worth discussing with a rehabilitation professional, who may be able to assess your imagery ability and tailor the approach, or advise that your energy is better spent elsewhere. Motor imagery is a useful tool, not a duty, and it is not the right tool for everyone.

Part Eight — In Closing

An honest closing word

It would be easy to end a page like this on a swell of optimism: to gather the encouraging studies, set aside the awkward ones, and leave you feeling that motor imagery is a quiet miracle waiting to be claimed. That would be a disservice, and it would betray the whole spirit of this programme.

Here is the fair account. Motor imagery is a real technique with a real scientific foundation. The idea that imagining movement engages the movement system is one of the better-established findings in the neuroscience of movement. Across spinal cord injury, stroke, Parkinson's disease, and multiple sclerosis, motor imagery has shown genuine benefits (for hand function, for walking, for fatigue, for daily independence) most reliably when added to physical rehabilitation and practised well.

And here are the limits, stated just as plainly. The studies are often small. They vary so much in design that firm, pooled conclusions are hard to reach. In Parkinson's, rigorous trials have found no effect at all on some symptoms. In spinal cord injury, a minority of people find that imagery increases their neuropathic pain. The ability to perform motor imagery is not guaranteed and can be affected by the very conditions it aims to help. And in no condition, not one, does the evidence support motor imagery as a cure or as a replacement for medical care and physical rehabilitation.

The programme this research supports is offered in that honest spirit: as a calm, low-cost, worthwhile practice, a serious complement to rehabilitation, never a substitute for it, and never a promise of recovery.

There is a particular reason to be careful with hope in this field. People living with a serious neurological condition are often, understandably, searching hard for something that might help, and that search can make anyone vulnerable to claims that promise more than they can deliver. The history of rehabilitation is littered with treatments that sounded wonderful and did little. A programme that respects the people it serves has a duty not to add to that pile. It has to resist the temptation to overstate, precisely because the people reading are motivated to believe.

So the kindest thing this page can do is also the most honest thing: to say clearly what motor imagery is and is not. It is a legitimate, well-studied technique that engages the movement system through the mind, that has shown real benefits across several conditions, and that is safe and accessible enough to be worth most people's trying. It is not a cure, not a substitute for medical care or physical therapy, not equally effective for everyone, and not free of the occasional downside, such as the increase in neuropathic pain that a minority of people with spinal cord injury experience. Both halves of that picture are true, and holding them together is not a hedge: it is the whole point.

Motor imagery asks very little of a person (no equipment, no exertion, no cost) and for many people it offers something real in return: a way to stay engaged with movement, to keep the motor system active, and to participate in their own rehabilitation even when the body is not cooperating. There is also something quietly valuable in that participation itself. To be an active agent in your own recovery, rather than a passive recipient of it, has its own worth, distinct from any measurable change in function. That is a worthwhile thing to offer, provided it is offered truthfully. Truthfully means with the benefits and the limits held together in the same hand. That is what this page has tried to do, and it is the standard the rest of this programme holds itself to.
